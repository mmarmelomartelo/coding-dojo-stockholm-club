What is Coding Dojo?

Just like martial arts, the dojo is an environment for learning. This concept must be reinforced to everyone participating in it. Besides that, it’s an environment where we apply the concepts of Pair Programming to solve a challenge given by the dojo master. The challenge resolution isn’t an obligation to call it a successful dojo; the main goal is to be a fun experience for everyone. A dojo can be done remotely, but the face-to-face one is where the magic happens.

Dojo Setup

A room or place with some seats Only one computer where people will code A big screen or projector where the crowd can see the coding

The Coding Dojo Flow

It has cycles of 5 to 7 minutes, which can be changed. Still, for beginners, it’s recommended that the cycle don’t be too big to avoid someone getting stuck at some logic and invite more people to go to the computer to code (I personally like it to be 5 minutes). The pilot is the only one who codes and can’t be interrupted by the audience during a cycle. The pair programming is happening with the co-pilot, the only person allowed to give opinions about the code. The audience remains quiet until the end of each cycle. Some people like to make quick pauses when the tests are passing to discuss something with the audience, which is fine. Still, when the tests fail, any audience interaction with the pilot and co-pilot is not allowed. At the break of each cycle, both pilot and co-pilot share their toughs of what they are doing with the audience. The pilot goes back to the audience, the co-pilot goes to the pilot seat, and someone from the audience (preferably who has not coded yet) assumes the co-pilot seat voluntarily. That will repeat all along with the dojo until the challenge is solved or the dojo time is up (around 40 minutes to one hour and a half).

Pair Programming

Pair Programming is a technique used at the dojo and at programming environments to improve teamwork and share knowledge. It is basically two people working together at a computer to solve a problem by talking about it, giving opinions on the implementation, helping to remember how to do something, and collaborating as a team towards the solution.

Finishing the Coding Dojo

At the end of e dojo, it’s a good practice to get all the pros and cons about that experience and get suggestions about what can be done to improve the next dojo session. All the participants get their names on the dojo list, and the code and the feedback are posted in a public repo to inspire others to make their own dojos.

Conclusion

At the dojo, the most important thing is to learn and have fun learning, this was just some tips from my experience at Coding Dojos.
